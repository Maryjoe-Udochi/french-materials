How to use this guide

This file is designed as a practical learning reference. The verb table uses the je form for each tense because it keeps the table easy to search and compare. For impersonal verbs like falloir, the natural form is shown as il faut.

Important: French has many irregular verbs and agreement rules. This guide focuses on high-use beginner/intermediate forms. For writing that must be perfect, double-check rare irregular verbs and past-participle agreement.

When to use each verb section

PrésentUse for now, habits, facts, and general truths.
Je parle français. = I speak French.
Subject + present verb
Passé composéUse for completed past actions or events. It usually means “did / have done.”
J’ai parlé hier. = I spoke yesterday.
avoir/être + past participle
Subjonctif présentUse after many expressions of necessity, emotion, doubt, desire, judgment, or uncertainty. It often comes after que.
Il faut que je parle. = I have to speak.
Il faut que / Je veux que / Bien que + subjunctive
ImparfaitUse for past habits, background descriptions, ongoing past situations, age/time/weather in the past.
Je parlais souvent français. = I used to speak French often.
Subject + imperfect verb
Conditionnel présentThis is the present conditional. Use it for “would,” polite requests, advice, or hypothetical results.
Je parlerais si j’avais le temps. = I would speak if I had time.
Si + imparfait, conditionnel
Futur simpleUse for future actions, promises, predictions, or more formal future statements.
Je parlerai demain. = I will speak tomorrow.
Subject + future verb
Futur procheUse for near future or planned actions. It is very common in speech.
Je vais parler. = I am going to speak.
aller au présent + infinitive
Plus-que-parfaitUse for an action that happened before another past action. It means “had done.”
J’avais parlé avant de partir. = I had spoken before leaving.
avoir/être in imparfait + past participle

Useful beginner structures

  • Je veux + infinitive: Je veux apprendre. = I want to learn.
  • Je dois + infinitive: Je dois étudier. = I must study.
  • Je peux + infinitive: Je peux parler. = I can speak.
  • Il faut + infinitive: Il faut pratiquer. = It is necessary to practise.
  • Il faut que + subjunctive: Il faut que je pratique. = I need to practise.
  • Si + imparfait, conditionnel: Si j’avais le temps, je voyagerais. = If I had time, I would travel.

French articles: when to use them

Definite articles: le, la, l’, lesUse when you mean “the,” or when speaking generally about a category.
Le café est chaud. = The coffee is hot.
J’aime la musique. = I like music.
Indefinite articles: un, une, desUse when you mean “a/an” or “some” countable things.
J’ai un livre. = I have a book.
Elle a des idées. = She has ideas.
Partitive articles: du, de la, de l’, desUse for an unspecified amount of food, drink, or abstract things.
Je bois de l’eau. = I drink water.
Je veux du riz. = I want some rice.
After negatives: de / d’After ne…pas, articles often become de/d’.
Je n’ai pas de voiture. = I don’t have a car.
Je ne bois pas d’eau. = I don’t drink water.

How to identify masculine and feminine nouns

The most reliable method is to learn the noun with its article: le livre, la table, l’eau. Endings can help, but there are exceptions.

Often masculine-age, -ment, -eau, -isme, -ier, -oir
Examples: le fromage, le bâtiment, le bateau, le tourisme.
Often feminine-tion, -sion, -té, -ette, -ance, -ence, -ure, -ade, -ie
Examples: la question, la décision, la liberté, la voiture.
Vowel nounsUse l’ before a vowel sound, so the article may hide gender. Check the dictionary or plural adjective agreement.
l’homme = masculine; l’eau = feminine
People and professionsGender often follows the person: un ami / une amie, un étudiant / une étudiante. Some job titles have special forms.
